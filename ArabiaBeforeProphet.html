Arabia before Prophet Mohammad

Sunday; October 1, 2017

Introduction

Arabia before the birth of Prophet Muhammad had been a culturally isolated and economically underdeveloped region. The region had few big towns ( Mecca & Yathrib ) and some small ones were situated in the north and south of the region. The rest of the country had a floating population composed of Bedouins also there was no state, only scattered independent tribes and towns.

All Arabs were notorious for certain characteristics such as arrogance, conceit, boastfulness, vindictiveness and excessive love of plunder.

Their arrogance was partly responsible for their failure to establish a state of their own, they also lacked political discipline, and until the rise of Islam, never acknowledged any authority as paramount in Arabia.

Arabia had a Caravan Culture with trade as a dominating factor of economy , also importent to note is that society was patriarchal and women didn’t have most of the basic rights.

Pre-Islamic Arabs were mostly polytheist and each tribe had their own deity.

Economic Condition

Economically, the Jewish people were the leaders of Arabia. They were the owners of the best arable lands and were the best farmers in the country. They were also the entrepreneurs of such industries as existed in Arabia in those days . Slavery was an economic institution of the Arabs. Male and female slaves were sold and bought like animals, and they formed the most depressed class of the Arabian society. 

The most powerful class of the Arabs was made up by the capitalists and money-lenders. They charged exorbitant rates of interest on loans, which made them richer and richer, and the borrowers poorer and poorer.  The most important urban centres of Arabia were Mecca and Yathrib. 

The citizens of Mecca were mostly merchants, traders and money-lenders.  Their caravans travelled in summer to Syria and in winter to Yemen. They also travelled to Bahrain in the east and to Iraq in the northeast. The caravan trade was basic to the economy of Mecca, and its organization called for considerable skill, experience and ability.

Social Condition

Arabia was a male-dominated society and women had no status other than that of a sex objects. There was a savage tradition of Arabs to bury their female infants alive. Even if an Arab did not wish to bury his daughter alive, he still had to uphold this “honourable” tradition, being unable to resist social pressures. 

The number of women a man could marry was not fixed, one more strange thing to note was that when a man died, his son “inherited” all his wives except his own mother. 

There was many type of marriages including the one which is prevalent today and:

 Nikah al-Istibda

A man temporarily sends his wife to a noble man so he could get a noble child.

 Nikah al-Istibda

The child is assigned to the man whom she points out and no one can refuse it.

Ten or more men are married to a woman

When a child is born they would sit together and summon the traditional doctor who assign the child to one of them.

State of Religion

Polytheists: Most of the Arabs worshipped numerous idols and each tribe had its own deity and idols. They had about 360 idols of stone and wood in Kaaba in Mecca.

Atheists: They were materialists and didn’t believe in any God.

Zindiqs: They were influenced by the Persian doctrine of dualism in nature and believed that there were two gods representing the twin forces of good and evil or light and darkness who were locked up in an unending struggle for supremacy.

Sabines: They worshipped the stars.

Jewish: They came to Arabia when the Romans destroyed Jerusalem and drove them out of Palestine and Syria in A.D. 70. Many Arabs under their influence converted to Judaism. Their strong centres were the towns of Yathrib, Khayber, Fadak and Umm-ul-Qura

Christians: In the north Arabian tribe of Ghassan were converted to Christianity by Romans. Some of their clans migrated and settled in Hijaz. In the south Christians was originally brought by the Ethiopian invaders. Their strong center was the town of Najran.

Monotheists: There was a small group of monotheists present in Arabia. They were the followers of the Prophet Abraham and didn’t worship idols. 

Education

Among the Arabs there were extremely few individuals who could read and write. Most of them were not very eager to learn these arts. The culture of the period was almost entirely oral and the Jews and Christians were the custodians of such knowledge. The greatest intellectual accomplishment of the pagan Arabs was their poetry. The greatest compositions of the pagan Arabs were called “Golden Odes,” a collection of seven poems, supposedly of unsurpassed excellence in spontaneity, power and eloquence. They were suspended in Kaaba as a challenge to any aspiring genius to excel or to match them.  

These poems had beauty of the language and wild richness but the subject of the poet was limited.

The popular themes were the charm of his mistress, his generosity and prowess, the unrivalled glory of his tribe, the noble qualities of his camel , some of them only added fuel to the besetting vices of the people, vainglory, envy, vindictiveness and pride
